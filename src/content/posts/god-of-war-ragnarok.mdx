ragnarok is the best-written video game sequel i've played in a decade and i'm going to die on this hill.

not the combat. combat is fine. prettier than 2018, technically more varied, but you beat the same four enemy archetypes into the same four piles of ichor for 40 hours and no one is writing dissertations on the runic attacks.

the writing is what makes it. and specifically — **mimir**. the severed head attached to kratos's belt. a companion character whose primary agency is running his mouth. he should be a gimmick. he should be the assassin's-creed-ikaros of this game, a utility function with a personality.

instead he's the best-written character in the game, and it's because the writers understood something most games miss: **if your companion can't shut up, let them actually say things.**

## the ambient dialogue problem

most open-world games have one solution to long traversal: a companion comments on the scenery. "hey kratos, look — a rune-marked stone." "interesting wildlife." "this place reminds me of... my home realm." it's filler. it's there because silence feels broken.

mimir has the structural luxury of being a head in a bag. he can talk during combat. he can talk during climbing. he can interrupt kratos mid-brood. and the writers use this bandwidth to actually develop him — we learn his history, his regrets, his jokes — in the gaps between setpieces, not *instead* of them.

by the time you're halfway through the game, the ambient banter between mimir, atreus, and kratos is functioning as a three-hander domestic drama. three men on a road trip with incompatible trauma. this is what the ps5 is for.

## what this has to do with software

i named [an agent after mimir](./jonah-brute-force-sql). the other agent i'm building is also named mimir. i couldn't think of a better metaphor for "knowledgeable thing permanently attached to the thing that actually acts." and the name stuck because it turns out every agent framework wants a mimir — a long-running, always-on-hand context, more conversational than a vector store, more specific than a system prompt.

this is probably a stretch. but i also think half of software naming is stretches that stuck.

## one criticism

the endgame drags. you finish the main quest and then the game asks if you'd like to do 14 more hours. no, ragnarok. i wouldn't. kratos is tired and so am i.

but [finishing this game at all](./why-i-cant-finish-things) was a minor personal victory. so i'm going easy on it.
