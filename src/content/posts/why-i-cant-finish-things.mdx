i have 34 unfinished projects on this laptop. i counted, because counting is easier than finishing any of them.

they are in various states. a drum machine in rust, abandoned at the point where i had to decide how to serialize patterns. a python cli that generates birthday card text, technically functional, never packaged. a novel in three chapters, one of which i like. an ios app about sleep that i stopped working on when i realized it would need a server. a fantasy map generator. a fantasy map. just a png.

none of these are bad ideas. most of them are, in fact, the same kind of okay-to-good that every unfinished project has ever been. the problem is not the ideas.

### where i actually stop

i used to think i stopped at the "hard part" — the technical crux, the plot beat i couldn't crack, the design problem. but when i look back honestly, that's not it. i push through those. the hard part is often the fun part.

where i stop is the **packaging part**. the part after the fun is over and the project has to become a thing someone else could hold. the readme. the screenshots. the copy. the deploy. the explanation, over and over, of what this is. the shipping.

### why that matters

because the *interesting* part of making something is figuring it out, and once it's figured out, the interesting part is *over*. what remains is transcription — translating the thing in your head into a thing outside your head that other people can see. and transcription is tedious. it's also where most of the value actually lives.

i've been thinking about this because [i actually finished something recently](./jonah-brute-force-sql) — a small tool, really small, but i wrote it up, put it somewhere people could find it, and two people used it. two. and that was more than the 34 unfinished projects combined, because two is more than zero.

### what i'm trying

three things, none of them original:

1. **start projects with the readme.** one paragraph, before any code. if i can't write the paragraph, the project isn't real yet.
2. **finish one before starting another.** this is obvious and i have broken it three times this month.
3. **[say what i'm working on out loud](./sunday-notes)**, even when it's stupid. public pressure is cheap. it works.

### the kratos thing

this is probably why i liked god of war ragnarok so much. kratos finishes things. kratos has a list and kratos completes the list, even when the list is generational and the items are his own regrets. i don't want kratos's life but i do want kratos's follow-through.

maybe that's the lesson. maybe i'm in my transcription era and i just have to sit through it.
